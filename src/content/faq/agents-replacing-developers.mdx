# Are agents replacing developers?

**Short version: no. Honest version: they're changing the shape of the job.**

## What's actually happening

Three things are true at once in 2026:

1. **Agents are doing *parts* of developer work.** Boilerplate code, refactors, test writing, docs, the painful 30% of every task that everyone always wanted to skip — agents do that meaningfully better than humans, faster, and at lower marginal cost.
2. **The remaining 70% has gotten harder.** System design, ambiguous requirements, debugging across systems, deciding what NOT to build, communicating with non-technical stakeholders. Those parts didn't get easier — they got more demanding because the easy parts got automated, raising the bar on the hard parts.
3. **Some companies are using ``agents'' as cover for layoffs.** Klarna in 2024–25 talked publicly about replacing customer-support roles with AI; the measured outcome was more nuanced (some efficiency gains, some quality regressions, partial walkback). DPD's chatbot (Jan 2024) became a meme for a reason. The rhetoric overran the reality.

The honest read: developer-replacement claims are mostly business-cycle messaging. The actual practice — for any company shipping software successfully in 2026 — is that good developers are using agents as multipliers and getting more done.

## What developers should actually do

If you're early in your career, three priorities:

1. **Get fluent with agents NOW.** Not ``ChatGPT can write your code'' fluency — *real fluency*. Build with MCPs. Run Computer Use workflows. Understand prompt injection. Read this site (and others).
2. **Lean into the parts agents can't do.** Architecture decisions. Cross-team communication. Customer empathy. Debugging the things AI tools can't reach.
3. **Pick a domain.** General developers were always at risk of commoditisation — that's now sharper. ``I know agents AND I know fintech'' is much more valuable than ``I know agents.''

If you're senior: same priorities, plus *teach the people coming in*. The senior developer who can pair with juniors using agents productively is doing the highest-value work in most teams right now.

## The career-shape change

The skill curve has steepened. The bottom got chopped off (basic syntax help, simple debugging — agents do it). The top got higher (system thinking, judgement, leadership — humans needed more than ever). The middle is where most discomfort lives.

If you're in the middle, the path is up, not sideways.
